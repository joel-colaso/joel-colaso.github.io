Ingenuity Inspired Drone

Ingenuity is a small helicopter landed by NASA on Mars in 2020 that has two coaxial helices and took images of Mars during 72 missions blowing the expected 5 out of the water.

During my second year at E.N.S.E.A. I worked alongside 3 of my peers, Kevin Dugard, Elio Flandin, and Pénélope Henner to design and build a drone inspired but made to fly on on Earth's atmosphere

Briefly, our drone used a Raspberry Pi Zero card to receive instructions by WiFi (SSH) from the GUI in a computer. Then it passed the instructions to a STM32 microcontroller which then decoded it and then controlled two motors and used a distance sensor to control the height of the helicopter.

My main responsability was writing the code for controlling the helicopter with the STM32 microcontroller

I designed the instruction format for communication between the Raspberry PI and the STM32 microcontrollers and developped the UART reception and decoding of the instructions.

I also developped the firmware to control the motors, which employed Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) to determine the power of the motors.

Here is an image of the final appearance of our chassis. The bases employ laser cutting and the other parts were 3D printed and painted.

One of the functionalities I implemented was the ability to react to height changes to control the drone's vertical position.

Here is a video of the pulse signal sent to the motor adapting to the height detected by the TOF sensor. Here, the selected control mode is "constant" (it adds or substracts a constant from the motor's command depending on whether detected height is over a threshold -the instruction height-) but I also implemented "linear" and "linear-differential" modes.

The following video is from one of the remote flight tests we performed. As a security measure, a member of the group (Kevin) used rods to limit movement if necessary while I controlled the drone's height using our graphic interface.

I used the deface Python tool to blur my colleagues' faces to respect their privacy.

Machine learning with Pytorch

Here is a Lab Report that I worked on following a practical school project on using neural networks for image compression and denoising.

It involved writing an autoencoder structure that's composed of an encoder, which connects the input layer to a more compact latent space where the image is compressed, and a decoder, that attempts to reconstruct the original image from the latent representation. First, we used a neural network composed only of linear and activation layers, and then we added a convolutional layer and compared the results. Among other conclusions, we confirmed that a convolutional model proves to be more adapted to learning to process 2D images.

This work was done in collaboration with my colleague Danilo Del Río Cisneros.
